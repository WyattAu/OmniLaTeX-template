\section*{Abstract}
Natural language processing (NLP) has advanced rapidly for high-resource
languages, yet over 7000 languages worldwide remain under-served.  This review
systematically examines transfer learning techniques that adapt models trained on
resource-rich languages to low-resource settings, organising the literature into
five thematic strands: cross-lingual pre-training, few-shot and zero-shot
adaptation, data augmentation strategies, evaluation frameworks, and multilingual
benchmarks.  Applying a PRISMA-compliant search protocol, we identified 87
primary studies from an initial pool of 2431 records, identifying critical gaps
in evaluation methodology, script diversity, and oral language support.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
The success of large pretrained language models such as
BERT~\cite{devlin2019bert}, GPT~\cite{brown2020gpt3}, and
XLM-R~\cite{conneau2020xlmr} has transformed NLP for high-resource languages.
Yet the vast majority of the world's languages lack sufficient annotated data to
train comparable models from scratch~\cite{jurafsky2023speech}.  As
\textcite{bender2021stochastic} argue, concentrating NLP resources in fewer than
20 languages creates a linguistic divide with significant societal consequences.
Transfer learning bridges this gap by leveraging knowledge from data-rich
languages for target languages with limited resources.

This review addresses three research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \label{rq:methods} What transfer learning methods are most effective
          for low-resource NLP tasks?
    \item \label{rq:generalise} How do cross-lingual representations generalise
          across typologically diverse languages?
    \item \label{rq:evaluate} What evaluation standards are appropriate for
          assessing model performance in low-resource settings?
\end{enumerate}

\Cref{sec:methodology} describes the PRISMA-compliant search protocol.
\Sectionsref{sec:crosslingual}--\ref{sec:evaluation} present the five thematic
strands.  \Cref{sec:synthesis} synthesises findings and identifies gaps.
\Cref{sec:conclusion} concludes with future directions and implications.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
We followed PRISMA 2020 guidelines~\cite{page2021prisma} to ensure
reproducibility.  \Cref{fig:prisma} presents the screening flow diagram.

\subsection{Search Strategy}
We queried five databases---ACL Anthology, dblp, Scopus, Web of Science, and
arXiv---using a Boolean query combining terms for transfer learning, low-resource
languages, and NLP tasks:

\begin{quote}
\texttt{("transfer learning" OR "cross-lingual" OR "multilingual") AND
("low-resource" OR "under-resourced") AND ("NLP" OR "natural language
processing" OR "language model")}
\end{quote}

The search covered January 2018--December 2024, yielding 2431 records after
deduplication.

\subsection{Screening and Selection}
Title and abstract screening eliminated 2187 records.  Full-text assessment of
the remaining 244 records excluded 157 studies that did not meet the inclusion
criteria (e.g., no empirical evaluation, fewer than two target languages).  The
final corpus comprises 87 primary studies.  Inter-rater reliability, measured
by Cohen's $\kappa$, was 0.84, indicating substantial agreement.

\section{Cross-Lingual Pre-Training}
\label{sec:crosslingual}
Cross-lingual pre-training encodes shared linguistic knowledge into a single
model, enabling zero-shot transfer between languages without parallel corpora.

\subsection{Multilingual Language Models}

representations transferable across 104

\Textcite{conneau2020xlmr} extended this to 100+ languages with XLM-RoBERTa,

of both universal syntactic properties and language-specific
regularities~\cite{chi2020empirical}.  However, as \parencite{lauscher2020length}
note, this comes with substantial computational cost.

\section{Few-Shot and Zero-Shot Adaptation}
\label{sec:fewshot}
When even a small number of labelled examples are available, parameter-efficient
fine-tuning can substantially improve upon zero-shot baselines.

\subsection{Prompt-Based Methods}
Large language models can be prompted without explicit fine-tuning.  Pattern-
exploiting training (PET)~\cite{schick2021pet} and contextual prompting achieve
competitive results with as few as 32 labelled examples per class.
\Textcite{gao2021calibrate} demonstrate that prompt engineering reduces the gap
between zero-shot and fully supervised performance by 40\% on XTREME.

\subsection{Cross-Lingual Transfer}
Zero-shot cross-lingual transfer fine-tunes on a high-resource source and
applies directly to a target language.  As shown in \cref{tab:cross-lingual},
performance correlates strongly with typological similarity between the
source--target pair~\cite{ponti2021xcopa,chi2020empirical}.

\section{Data Augmentation Strategies}
\label{sec:augmentation}
Data augmentation addresses the fundamental scarcity of labelled examples by
generating synthetic training instances.

\subsection{Translation-Based Augmentation}
Translating labelled data into the target language is straightforward but can
introduce noise from translation artefacts and domain
mismatch~\cite{zhang2020backtranslation}.  Back-translation and neural
paraphrase generation are effective alternatives, particularly for
morphologically rich languages where inflectional variation is
substantial~\cite{siddhant2020meta,tran2021improving}.  As
\textcite{kobak2021cross} observe, back-translation consistently outperforms
simple translation by 2--4 F1 points across language pairs.

\subsection{LLM-Based Augmentation}
Large language models offer scalable augmentation through controlled text
generation.  \Cref{tab:augmentation-compare} compares augmentation strategies.

\label{sec:evaluation}
Rigorous evaluation is essential for meaningful progress, yet existing
frameworks present significant limitations.

\subsection{Evaluation Challenges}
Held-out test sets are scarce in low-resource settings.  Cross-validation and
stratified sampling are common but may overestimate out-of-sample
performance~\cite{blitzstein2021sample}.  Furthermore, as
\parencite{geiger2024evaluating} demonstrate, many benchmarks suffer from
annotation artefacts that inflate apparent model capabilities.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
This review systematically examined transfer learning for low-resource NLP
through a PRISMA-compliant methodology, identifying 87 studies across five
thematic strands.

\subsection{Research Gaps}
The analysis reveals four persistent gaps:
\begin{description}
    \item[Script diversity] Most models assume shared scripts or Latin
    transliteration, limiting applicability for Ge'ez, Tibetan, or Brahmic
    scripts~\cite{lauscher2020length}.
    \item[Oral languages] Languages with limited written traditions are largely
    absent, perpetuating linguistic
    inequality~\cite{bender2021stochastic}.
    \item[Evaluation standards] No consensus exists for extremely low-resource
    settings (< 1000 labelled sentences)~\cite{geiger2024evaluating}.
    \item[Efficiency] Models exceeding 1B parameters preclude deployment on
    resource-constrained devices common in low-resource regions.
\end{description}

\subsection{Future Directions}
Future research should prioritise:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Inclusive evaluation methodologies} accounting for
    typological diversity and oral language traditions.
    \item \textbf{Efficient architectures} via distillation, quantisation, and
    sparse mixture-of-experts for modest hardware.
    \item \textbf{Community-driven dataset development} centring the needs and
    agency of speakers of under-resourced languages.
    \item \textbf{Unified multilingual benchmarks} providing fair evaluation
    across diverse scripts, language families, and task types.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Implications}
Transfer learning has substantially extended NLP to low-resource languages, but
equitable advances require deliberate attention to the gaps identified herein.
The field must move beyond performance metrics on well-resourced languages toward
genuine linguistic inclusivity.
