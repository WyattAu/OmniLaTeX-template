\textbf{THE LAST LIBRARY}\\[0.5cm]
\textit{An Original Screenplay}\\[0.3cm]
by\\[0.3cm]
James Hartwell\\[1cm]
Draft: June 2024\\
WGA Registration No.: 1894532
\end{center}

\vspace{1cm}

\section*{LOGLINE}

In a near-future where all books have been digitized and physical copies outlawed, a retired librarian discovers that the last surviving library holds more than forgotten books---it holds the key to restoring human memory itself.

\section*{CHARACTERS}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ELEANOR VOSS} (70s) --- A retired librarian. Sharp mind, stubborn spirit. Carries the weight of a lifetime spent protecting stories.
    \item \textbf{MARCUS CHEN} (30s) --- A government compliance officer. Efficient, conflicted. Ordered to shut down the last library.
    \item \textbf{SARAH VOSS} (40s) --- Eleanor's daughter. A digital rights activist. Loves her mother but disagrees with her methods.
    \item \textbf{THE ARCHIVIST} (ageless) --- A mysterious figure who has guarded the library for longer than anyone can remember.
\end{itemize}

\section*{SCREENPLAY}

\begin{center}
\textbf{FADE IN:}
\end{center}

\section*{EXT. CITY STREETS --- DAWN}

A gray city stretches to the horizon. Buildings of glass and steel reflect the pale morning light. Digital billboards display messages in crisp, bright text:

\begin{quote}
\textit{DIGITAL READERSHIP ACT --- COMPLIANCE IS MANDATORY}\\
\textit{PHYSICAL MEDIA OFFENSES: MINIMUM 5 YEARS}\\
\textit{REPORT ILLEGAL BOOK OWNERSHIP: 1-800-555-READ}
\end{quote}

Pedestrians walk the streets, each wearing sleek AR glasses. Their eyes move back and forth, reading invisible text projected onto their retinas. No one carries a bag. No one holds a paper. No one looks up.

A DELIVERY DRONE buzzes overhead, dropping a package at a doorstep. The package is thin, flat, unmistakable: a new e-reader. A CHILD (10) picks it up, unwraps it with practiced fingers, and puts on the glasses that come with it. Her eyes light up---literally---as the device activates.

We PAN UP from the street, past the buildings, past the billboards, to a small rooftop garden on an older building. Among the vegetables and flowers, a single WINDOW stands open, warm light spilling from inside.

\section*{INT. ELEANOR'S APARTMENT --- CONTINUOUS}

The apartment is a time capsule. Bookshelves line every wall, packed with physical books---thousands of them, spines cracked, pages yellowed. This is illegal. Every book in this room is a felony.

ELEANOR VOSS (70s) sits at a small desk, reading a physical book by lamplight. Her glasses---actual glasses, not AR-enabled---sit low on her nose. She turns a page with the careful reverence of someone handling something precious.

The book is \textit{ ``The Complete Poems of Emily Dickinson.''} Eleanor reads aloud, softly:

\begin{quote}
\textit{``There is no Frigate like a Book\\
To take us Lands away...''}
\end{quote}

A KNOCK at the door. Eleanor looks up. She closes the book, places it in a drawer, and locks it with a small brass key. She smooths her hair, straightens her cardigan, and walks to the door.

\begin{quote}
ELEANOR\\
One moment.
\end{quote}

She opens the door. MARCUS CHEN (30s) stands in the hallway, wearing a gray government uniform. His badge reads: \textit{``DIGITAL COMPLIANCE DIVISION.''}

Behind him, two ENFORCEMENT OFFICERS stand at attention, their faces blank, their AR glasses reflecting the fluorescent hallway lights.

\begin{quote}
MARCUS\\
Mrs. Voss? I'm Officer Chen,\\
Digital Compliance Division.\\
May I come in?

ELEANOR\\
I suppose you can.\\
You're already here.
\end{quote}

She steps aside. Marcus enters. He looks around the apartment, and his eyes widen. The books. Everywhere, books.

He reaches out and touches a spine---gently, as though it might bite him.

\begin{quote}
MARCUS\\
How long have you had these?

ELEANOR\\
Some of them? Since before\\
you were born. The Dickinson\\
was my mother's. The Dickens\\
collection belonged to my\\
grandfather. The Austen first\\
editions---\\

MARCUS\\
First editions?

ELEANOR\\
I'm a librarian, Officer Chen.\\
Or I was, before they decided\\
librarians were subversive.
\end{quote}

Marcus pulls out a TABLET and begins scanning the room. Red dots appear on the screen, one for each book. The count climbs: 847, 1,203, 1,891...

\begin{quote}
MARCUS\\
Mrs. Voss, you're aware\\
that physical media ownership\\
is a violation of the Digital\\
Readership Act?

ELEANOR\\
I'm aware that you're aware,\\
which means someone told you\\
I had books. Which means\\
someone betrayed me. Which\\
means I'd like to know who.
\end{quote}

Marcus lowers the tablet. He looks at Eleanor, and something shifts in his expression. Not pity. Not quite guilt. Something else.

\begin{quote}
MARCUS\\
My grandmother had books.\\
Before the Act. She kept them\\
in a closet, behind the winter\\
coats. When I was a child,\\
she would read to me\\
from them. Real books.\\
With pages you could\\
actually turn.

ELEANOR\\
What happened to them?

MARCUS\\
They were taken. During\\
the Second Purge. My grandmother\\
was fined. She lost her job.\\
She never read again.
\end{quote}

A long silence. Eleanor studies Marcus's face.

\begin{quote}
ELEANOR\\
And now you work for them.

MARCUS\\
I work for the system.\\
There's a difference.

ELEANOR\\
Is there?
\end{quote}

Marcus doesn't answer. He looks at the books again, at the thousands of stories bound in paper and ink, and his jaw tightens.

\begin{quote}
MARCUS\\
I have to report this.\\
You know that.

ELEANOR\\
You have to do your job.\\
I understand. But before you do,\\
I'd like to show you something.

MARCUS\\
What?

ELEANOR\\
The library.
\end{quote}

\begin{center}
\textbf{CUT TO:}
\end{center}

\section*{INT. APARTMENT BASEMENT --- MOMENTS LATER}

Eleanor leads Marcus down a narrow staircase. The basement is larger than expected, extending back into shadow. Eleanor flips a switch, and fluorescent lights flicker on, revealing...

A library.

Not a room of books. A \textit{library}. Shelves stretch from floor to ceiling, arranged in aisles that disappear into the darkness. A card catalog sits in the center, its drawers labeled in handwriting. A wooden desk holds a vintage typewriter, a lamp, and a stack of handwritten index cards.

Marcus stops. He stares.

\begin{quote}
MARCUS\\
This is...

ELEANOR\\
The last one. The only one.\\
When they banned physical books,\\
we moved everything here.\\
Every book we could save.\\
Every story. Every poem.\\
Every piece of human knowledge\\
that they couldn't digitize\\
because it didn't fit\\
in their algorithms.

MARCUS\\
How many?

ELEANOR\\
Forty-seven thousand,\\
three hundred and twelve.\\
Give or take.
\end{quote}

Marcus walks slowly down one of the aisles, trailing his fingers along the spines. He pulls one out at random: \textit{ ``One Hundred Years of Solitude''} by Gabriel Garc\'{i}a M\'{a}rquez.

He opens it. The smell of old paper rises from the pages. He breathes it in.

\begin{quote}
MARCUS\\
I've never held one before.\\
Not since I was a child.

ELEANOR\\
A book is more than words,\\
Officer Chen. It's an object.\\
It's a weight in your hands.\\
It's the sound a page makes\\
when you turn it. It's the margin\\
notes someone left a hundred years ago,\\
arguing with the author.\\
You can't get that from a screen.

MARCUS\\
No. You can't.
\end{quote}

He puts the book back. He looks at Eleanor.

\begin{quote}
MARCUS\\
What do you want from me?

ELEANOR\\
I want you to look around.\\
I want you to read something.\\
I want you to remember\\
what we've lost. And then\\
I want you to decide\\
what kind of world\\
you want to live in.
\end{quote}

Marcus looks at the shelves, at the tens of thousands of books, at the accumulated wisdom and folly and beauty of the human race, preserved here in paper and ink.

He sits down at the desk. He picks up a book from the stack: \textit{ ``The Great Gatsby''} by F. Scott Fitzgerald.

He opens it to the first page.

He begins to read.

\begin{center}
\textbf{SMASH CUT TO:}
\end{center}

\section*{INT. DIGITAL COMPLIANCE HEADQUARTERS --- DAY}

A vast, sterile office. Rows of identical desks. Screens glow everywhere. Officers sit in perfect rows, their AR glasses reflecting data streams.

DIRECTOR ANNA WALSH (50s) stands at the front of the room, addressing the assembled officers. Behind her, a massive screen displays the day's mandate:

\begin{quote}
\textit{OPERATION LAST CHAPTER\\
TARGET: 742 OAK STREET\\
OBJECTIVE: FULL SEIZURE AND DESTRUCTION\\
PRIORITY: CRITICAL}
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
DIRECTOR WALSH\\
Good morning. As you know,\\
we have located the final\\
repository of illegal physical media\\
in this city. The target is\\
a former public library,\\
converted into an unauthorized\\
storage facility.\\[0.3cm]
The owner is Eleanor Voss,\\
age seventy-three, former head\\
librarian of the Manhattan\\
Public Library, retired 2019.\\
She has been flagged as\\
a Category One subversive\\
for the past six years.\\[0.3cm]
Our intelligence indicates\\
that she is harboring\\
approximately fifty thousand\\
physical media items,\\
including books, manuscripts,\\
and printed documents\\
of significant ideological value\\
to anti-digital resistance groups.\\[0.3cm]
This is unacceptable.\\
The Digital Readership Act\\
exists for a reason.\\
Physical media is inefficient,\\
environmentally destructive,\\
and ideologically dangerous.\\
It promotes independent thought,\\
critical analysis, and emotional\\
engagement---all of which\\
undermine social stability.\\[0.3cm]
We will move tonight.\\
Zero hour is 0200.\\
Full seizure. No exceptions.\\
Any questions?
\end{quote}

A HUNDRED officers stare back. No one speaks.

\begin{quote}
DIRECTOR WALSH\\
Good. Dismissed.
\end{quote}

The officers file out. Director Walsh remains, watching them go. Her face is unreadable.

She turns to the screen. The target address glows in red: \textit{742 Oak Street.}

She touches the screen, and a new message appears:

\begin{quote}
\textit{ASSET IN PLACE: CHEN, MARCUS\\
STATUS: ACTIVE\\
NEXT REPORT: 2200}
\end{quote}

Walsh smiles. It is not a warm smile.
